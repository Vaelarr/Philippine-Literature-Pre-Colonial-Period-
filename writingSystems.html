Baybayin

  One of the precolonial writing systems that the early Filipinos utilized was called baybayin. The Tagalog root word baybay, which meaning "to spell," is where the phrase "baybayin" originates due to the arrangement of another alphabet system, Arabic, whose initial letters are designated alif, ba, and ta, the script was mistakenly termed "alibata" for a long time the majority of the indigenous people of the Philippine archipelago, not only the elite, were literate when the Spaniards first arrived. In addition to recording, the script was used to write letters, poetry, and incantations.


  In technical terms, the Baybayin is classified as an alphasyllabary, where each character is derived from a consonant letter, with a corresponding vowel notation indicating the vowel sound. The system comprises 14 syllabic consonant characters (or 15 if the "da" and "ra" consonants are considered separately, as depicted in the images). Additionally, there are three vowel characters for "a," "e-i," and "o-u."




Baybayin Characters




  The consonant characters represent a combination of the consonant sound and the vowel sound “a.” To modify this to the “e-I” sound, a kudlit or mark is added on top of the character, while for the “o-u” sound, the mark is positioned at the bottom.

  The Spanish friars extensively examined Baybayin and utilized it as a tool for imparting Catholic teachings to the Filipino population. Notably, a section of the Doctrina Christiana, one of the earliest printed books in the country, incorporates both Baybayin script and Latinized Tagalog.

  To signify standalone consonants, the Spanish introduced a cross mark at the bottom of Baybayin characters. For instance, the original Baybayin representation of the word for God, "Bathala," was written as "Bahala." Employing this cross mark, Spanish writers employed a different character, "ta," and added a cross at the bottom to eliminate the "a" sound.
