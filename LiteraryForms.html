Literary Forms

  The literary forms in the pre-colonial period of the Philippines were predominantly oral in nature, as a writing system was not yet in place. Despite the absence of written records diverse indigenous communities across the archipelago had rich traditions of storytelling poetry, and song, which were vital for preserving cultural heritage and passing down knowledge through generations.

  One of the prominent literary forms was epic poetry, exemplified by works such as "Biag ni Lam-ang" from the Ilocano people and the "Hudhud" epic of the Ifugao. These narratives, often sung or chanted, recounted the heroic exploits of protagonists, encapsulating the cultural ethos and values of the communities. Proverbs and sayings, known as "Salawikain" or "Sawikain," were another significant literary expression. These concise expressions served as repositories of collective wisdom, imparting moral lessons and reflecting the shared experiences of the community.

  Riddles, in the form of "Bugtong," added an intellectual dimension to storytelling. These challenges, often containing metaphorical language, engaged individuals in creative thinking and provided a form of entertainment. Fables and folktales were prevalent, offering explanations for natural phenomena, teaching moral lessons, and providing entertainment. Animals frequently took on symbolic roles as characters in these narratives, contributing to the cultural richness of the oral tradition. Furthermore, traditional songs and dances were integral components of precolonial rituals and ceremonies, functioning as vehicles for storytelling, emotional expression, and spiritual invocation. These art forms underscored the interconnectedness of narrative and performance in conveying cultural narratives.

  Although certain native communities created written scripts like the Tagalog Baybayin and the Mangyan script, these were not as commonly used as oral traditions for communication. The literary scene in precolonial times showcased a rich diversity, representing various cultures and languages thriving in the Philippines. However, the substantial effects of colonization introduced new influences, altering the course of literary development in the region.
